Article Introduction

Our world is changing; for the elderly, semiconductors, the world wide web, mobile phones etc., were new inventions in their lifetime[cite: 72]. These brought severe changes, and technology developed rapidly[cite: 73]. Those left behind struggle to catch up and may resist learning new skills[cite: 74], widening the gap between them and the tech-savvy[cite: 75]. Post-pandemic, digitalization of services has increased[cite: 76], further merging physical and digital worlds. Without intervention, the elderly risk marginalization[cite: 77].

Article Summary

The digital divide refers to the gap older adults face in accessing information and communication technology (ICT)[cite: 79], leading to potential exclusion from digital society[cite: 80], a serious social issue[cite: 81]. Modern trends show increasing reliance on digital services for government functions, payments, and commerce[cite: 82, 83], often reducing in-person options[cite: 84, 85, 86, 87]. This shift disadvantages those lacking digital literacy or access, particularly the elderly[cite: 88, 89], and increases vulnerability to cybercrime[cite: 90]. Elderly individuals face challenges with complex applications[cite: 91, 92], device accessibility issues (eyesight, hearing)[cite: 93], and psychological barriers like feeling ICT is for the 'digital natives' [cite: 94] or lacking confidence[cite: 96, 99]. However, promoting ICT use among seniors offers benefits: it's becoming a vital life skill[cite: 100], enables self-sufficiency (e.g., online health services)[cite: 101], potentially reducing costs for support services[cite: 102, 103], and helps maintain social connections, easing loneliness[cite: 104, 105, 106]. Recommendations include developing elderly-friendly technology[cite: 108, 109, 110], conducting cross-sector research[cite: 111, 112, 113], providing effective ICT tutorials focusing on self-learning[cite: 114, 115, 116], training volunteers[cite: 117, 118], and encouraging family support[cite: 119, 120, 121, 122]. Addressing this digital divide is crucial for societal inclusion[cite: 107, 124, 125].

Text 1: Digital Marginalization of Older Adults
Stance: Digital marginalization significantly affects older adults, exacerbating their exclusion from a digitally dominated society[cite: 1].
Trends and Phenomena: Increased reliance on Information and Communication Technology (ICT) post-COVID-19[cite: 2]. Deepening digital divide between older and younger generations, with only 27.7% of seniors using online media compared to 83.0% of younger individuals[cite: 3].
Issues: Barriers such as economic constraints and inadequate access to technology[cite: 4]. Psychological factors, including anxiety and lack of confidence in using digital devices[cite: 5].
Recommendations: Acknowledge the urgent need for initiatives to bridge the digital divide for older adults[cite: 6].
Related Sentences: "Digitally marginalized individuals or groups are deprived of total participation in the life of a digital society." [cite: 7] "The rich are often equipped with state-of-the-art devices and high-speed internet connections, whereas the less privileged often face very limited access." [cite: 8]

Text 2: Impact of Regular Internet Use on Seniors’ Wellness
Stance: Regular internet use is essential for improving the wellness of older adults[cite: 9].
Trends and Phenomena: Digital skills are becoming necessary life skills for older adults[cite: 10]. Engagement in online activities correlates with reduced loneliness and improved mental well-being[cite: 11].
Issues: A significant portion of seniors (only 27%) use social networking sites regularly, leading to vulnerability to loneliness and depression[cite: 12].
Recommendations: Promote digital inclusion to alleviate social isolation and enhance the quality of life for seniors[cite: 13].
Related Sentences: "Digitally marginalized seniors...were found more vulnerable to loneliness and depression." [cite: 14] "Digital inclusion potentially alleviates social isolation and improves the mental well-being of older adults." [cite: 15]

Text 3: Research Field of Digital Marginalization of Older Adults
Stance: There is an increasing academic focus on the digital marginalization of older adults due to rising digital disparities[cite: 16].
Trends and Phenomena: A surge in publications regarding digital inclusion and marginalization since 2019, particularly post-pandemic[cite: 17].
Issues: Older adults face significant challenges in digital competence, impacting their participation in society[cite: 18].
Recommendations: More research and collaboration are needed to understand the needs of older adults in a digital context[cite: 19].

Text 4: Challenges of Launching Elderly Digital Education
Stance: Promoting digital education for older adults is crucial but faces several challenges[cite: 22].
Trends and Phenomena: Governments are intensifying efforts to improve digital inclusion as a national strategy[cite: 23].
Issues: Lack of technical support and professional training for young volunteers teaching older adults[cite: 24]. Many older adults remain dependent on traditional methods due to limited digital skills[cite: 25].
Recommendations: Provide better training for volunteers and develop more accessible digital education programs for older adults[cite: 26].
Related Sentences: "There is a lack of technical support and professional training for those young volunteers." [cite: 27] "Promoting digital inclusion has become every country’s national development strategy in the twenty-first century." [cite: 28]

T5 The Digital Divide Among Low-Income Homebound Older Adults:
Stances: The study emphasizes the significant digital divide affecting low-income homebound older adults, particularly those aged 60 and older[cite: 29]. These individuals are particularly vulnerable and often lack access to the Internet and digital resources[cite: 30]. Internet technology is portrayed as essential for managing health, maintaining social connections, and improving quality of life for homebound older adults[cite: 31].
Trends: Low Internet Use: Only 34% of those under 60 and 17% of those 60 and older were current Internet users[cite: 32], a substantial gap[cite: 33]. Discontinued Use: A considerable number reported discontinuing Internet use (35% under-60, 16% 60+) primarily due to cost and disabilities[cite: 34]. Demographics of Non-Users: Never users are more likely to be black or Hispanic, have lower incomes, and experience more chronic medical conditions[cite: 35].
Phenomena: eHealth Literacy: Low eHealth literacy among older adults is critical for navigating health information online [cite: 36] and is associated with poorer health outcomes[cite: 37]. Attitudes Toward Technology: Mixed attitudes, with many expressing anxiety and lack of confidence[cite: 38].
Issues: Barriers include lack of financial resources, physical disabilities, and absence of training[cite: 39]. Low-income homebound older adults face significant health challenges exacerbated by social isolation and lack of access to online health resources[cite: 40].
Recommendations: Implement training programs for eHealth literacy[cite: 41]. Increase access to affordable computers and Internet services[cite: 42]. Promote telehealth services[cite: 43]. Engage community organizations for support[cite: 44].
Related Sentences: "This study is the first to describe in detail low-income disabled and homebound adults’ and older adults’ Internet use." [cite: 45] "Internet technology can provide a diverse array of online resources for homebound older adults..." [cite: 46] "...reasons for not using computer/Internet technology: the cost... functional impairments..." [cite: 47] "Given vulnerable... adults’ substantial health and mental needs, examining their ability to search for... health information... may be particularly useful." [cite: 47] "Recommendations to reduce the digital divide... are provided." [cite: 48]
